HOPI TIES TO WATER

Tracing the Ties Between Hopi People, Spring and Public Spaces

Xiaoyou Guo
xiaoyou@upenn.edu

Hopi Reservations

The Hopi Reservation (Hopi: Hopitutskwa) is a Native American reservation for the Hopi and Arizona Tewa people, surrounded entirely by the Navajo Nation.

Located in northeastern Arizona, the Hopi Reservation is defined by an arid climate with scarce and unpredictable precipitation. Agriculture remains central to how Hopi people sustain life and maintain their relationship with the land, practiced through both dry farming reliant on rainfall and spring-irrigated gardens. Beyond rainfall, springs serve as the most critical and dependable water source for the Hopi.

The Vanishing Voice Of Hopi Springs

The Hopi mesas have long been shaped by water, with springs serving as essential sources of life, ceremony, and settlement. These water points not only sustain daily living but also anchor cultural practices and collective memory across generations. Understanding the distribution of springs offers insight into the environmental and social landscape that has guided the Hopi people for centuries.

The map below traces both the historical and present-day locations of springs across the mesas, revealing a pattern of loss and fragmentation. Yet the remaining springs remain vital markers of cultural memory and living connections between the Hopi people and water.

Three Springs, Three Stories

These three typical yet distinct Hopi springs were selected to tell the story of Hopi water sources across different landscapes. Tawapa Spring and Coyote Spring, both on First Mesa, reveal water-holding points that help construct community life on the mesa; they are accessible, culturally central, and closely tied to community rituals. Pipe Spring, located in the more remote and lush Keams Canyon, represents a very different type of water source and the landscape it nourishes. Together, these springs illustrate the diversity of Hopi water sources and cultural values, offering a window into how people interact with and adapt to the landscape.

Tawapa Spring: Gathering Center

Tawapa Spring exemplifies a typical Hopi spring, traditionally serving as a site for ceremonies and communal gatherings. Located on First Mesa near the old day school, the spring now sits beside an open, vacant ground that remains quiet yet central, preserving the memory of past gatherings. The surrounding area includes a terrace garden, play facilities, and is in close proximity to the community center. Today, while the spring no longer provides water for drinking or irrigation, it continues to mark an important cultural and social landmark within the community.

Coyote Spring: The Sacred Water With Eroding Edges

Coyote Spring is a unique underground spring located on First Mesa, near a sharply eroding water channel. Its entrance is marked by a stone structure, with numerous feathers placed at the doorway to signify its sacred importance. The spring’s immediate surroundings form a fragile boundary, where exposed soil and fading vegetation reflect ongoing erosion that threatens both the site’s physical stability and its cultural significance. Despite these vulnerabilities, Coyote Spring remains an important marker of spiritual and communal life, connecting the landscape with Hopi traditions.

Keams Canyon Pipe Spring: Hidden Life Source Beneath the Lush

Keams Canyon Pipe Spring lies deep within Keams Canyon, far from First Mesa and the Hopi villages. The canyon’s higher moisture levels support particularly lush vegetation, creating a vibrant landscape around the spring. Accessing the site requires driving into the canyon and then following the wetland slope fed by the spring, tracing the stream uphill through winding paths. The spring emerges from beneath the rocks in a small, secluded space, offering a hidden and tranquil setting. Few people visit this site regularly, though ceremonies continue to be held there. The dense vegetation and tree cover create a cool, shaded environment, making the spring’s small space well-suited for reflection and meditation.
